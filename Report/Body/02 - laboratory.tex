
\section{Laboratory Work and Experimental Results}

\subsection{Initial Hardware Testing}

\subsubsection{LQE Validation}

\par The first stage of the experimental procedure involved the validation of the LQE. 
To facilitate this, the LQG controller structure was decoupled into its two primary components: the 
LQR regulator and the LQE observer. This separation was implemented in the Simulink by
using independent blocks, as shown in the system diagram in Figure \ref{fig:lqr_plus_lqe}. This configuration 
allowed for the isolation of the estimator, enabling the monitoring of the observer's performance.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{Body/Images/LQR+LQE.png}
    \caption{Simulink implementation of the complete LQR + LQE architecture for hardware validation.}
    \label{fig:lqr_plus_lqe}
\end{figure}

\par The primary objective of this test was first, to ensure that the estimated states $\hat{\alpha}$ and
 $\hat{\beta}$ accurately tracked the raw angular measurements; and second, to verify that the 
remaining three unmeasured 
states behaved smoothly and remained strictly within expected physical boundaries, without exhibiting 
erratic fluctuations or excessive noise amplification. This objective was achieved by tuning the 
estimator's noise covariance matrices, $Q_e$ and $R_e$.

\par A fundamental observation regarding the experimental setup was the high precision of the 
discrete sensors. The Quanser platform utilizes a potentiometer for the rotary arm and a 
high-resolution optical encoder for the pendulum angle. Due to their discrete nature, the 
continuous measurement noise was found to be practically negligible. However, to ensure numerical 
stability in the Kalman Filter algorithm and to reflect a high degree of confidence in the hardware 
readings, the measurement noise covariance matrix was set to:

\begin{equation}
    R_e = I_2 \cdot (\text{deg2rad}(0.1))^2
\end{equation}

\par With $R_e$ fixed to admit a standard deviation of only $0.1^\circ$, the $Q_e$ matrix was 
tuned empirically through hardware trials. In the Kalman Filter framework, it is the relative ratio 
between these covariance matrices ($Q_e / R_e$) that dictates the observer's gain and dynamic 
behavior. A high ratio yields a highly reactive filter, whereas a low ratio produces a slower, 
heavily filtered response.

\par To find the optimal balance, ensuring accurate tracking while avoiding overfitting, 
three distinct tests were performed by scaling the process noise identity matrix keeping $R_e$ constant: 
$Q_e = 0.1 \cdot I_5$, $Q_e = I_5$, and $Q_e = 10 \cdot I_5$.

\par As illustrated in Figures \ref{fig:lqe_plots_q10}, \ref{fig:lqe_plots_qe1}, \ref{fig:lqe_plots_qe0_1}, the system's real-time plots were analyzed for each configuration. 
The results demonstrated that while lower values of $Q_e$ introduced a noticeable lag, the 
configuration with $Q_e = 10 \cdot I_5$ provided the best overall 
performance. It allowed the estimates $\hat{\alpha}$ and $\hat{\beta}$ to converge rapidly to the 
physical measurements with high fidelity. Crucially, the plots also confirmed that the three 
unmeasured states exhibited coherent dynamic trends, remaining well within the expected operational 
limits during the pendulum's motion. This thoroughly validated LQE setup was then adopted for all 
subsequent closed-loop experiments.

\subsubsection{LQG Testing}

\par Following the validation of the LQE, the complete LQG controller was implemented on the 
hardware. The weighting matrices for the LQR regulator, $Q_r$ and $R_r$, were selected based on the 
optimal values determined during the theoretical simulation phase. However, for this test, we decided
to maintain the default values of $Q_r = diag(10, 0, 1, 0, 0)$ and $R_r = 0.01$.

\par The explicit objective of this hardware test was to demonstrate a fundamental 
limitation of the standard LQG architecture: its inability to stabilize the rotary 
arm ($\alpha$) at a complete standstill at the origin.

\par To evaluate this, a physical disturbance was introduced to the system during operation. 
As illustrated in the experimental plots in Figure \ref{fig:lqg_default_pert}, while the controller successfully 
kept the pendulum upright, the rotary arm failed to return to the zero position. Instead, 
$\alpha$ exhibited a persistent steady-state error, drifting and coming to a stop away from 
the reference point.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.35\textwidth]{Body/Images/lqg_default.pdf}
    \caption{Default LQG response to a physical disturbance.}
    \label{fig:lqg_default_pert}
\end{figure}


\subsubsection{Analysis and Problems}

\par An analysis of this drift revealed two coupled causes rooted in the hardware's physical 
properties and the controller's structure:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Motor Deadzone: The DC motor only actuates when the applied voltage exceeds a specific 
    threshold. Within this deadzone range, the control inputs ($u$) calculated by the LQR do 
    absolutely nothing. As a consequence, the rotary arm will always exhibit a large amplitude of 
    oscillation. The system drifts until the position error grows large enough for the controller to 
    finally command a voltage that breaks through the deadzone, causing the motor to suddenly move, 
    overshoot, and repeat the cycle.

    \item Absence of an Integrative State: The problem of the steady-state drift is fundamentally 
    tied to the composition of our state vector. The standard LQR model relies strictly on the 
    proportional feedback of the position and velocity states. Because it lacks an integrative 
    state dedicated to accumulating the error of $\alpha$ over time, the controller has no 
    "memory." It inherently accepts steady-state offsets whenever the proportional control effort 
    generated by that offset is smaller than the deadzone resistance, causing the arm to settle 
    away from the reference point.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{LQG with Integral Action: Theory and Validation}

\par To eliminate the steady-state drift of the rotary arm, an integral action was added to 
the controller by introducing a new state associated with the angle $\alpha$. The purpose 
of this additional state is to accumulate the arm-position error over time, so that any 
persistent offset generates an increasing corrective action. In the original LQG formulation, 
the control law depended only on proportional feedback of the estimated states, which means 
that a constant bias or disturbance could be balanced by a non-zero steady-state value of $\alpha$. 
As a result, the pendulum could remain stabilized while the arm settled away from the origin. 
The added integrator removes this limitation by giving the controller memory of the accumulated 
error. This directly addresses the limitation identified in the previous subsection as the absence 
of an integrative state. 

\subsubsection{Theoretical Approach and Augmented State-Space}

\par Let the original state be $x \in \mathbb{R}^5$, and define the new integrator state as
\begin{equation}
\xi_{\alpha}(t)=\int_0^t \alpha(\tau)\,d\tau .
\end{equation}

\par Since the reference for $\alpha$ is zero, integrating $\alpha$ is equivalent, up to 
sign convention, to integrating the tracking error. Using the first output row of the 
measurement matrix,
\[
C_{\alpha}=C(1,:)=[\,1\ 0\ 0\ 0\ 0\,],
\]
the augmented state becomes
\begin{equation}
z=
\begin{bmatrix}
x\\
\xi_{\alpha}
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
\dot{\xi}_{\alpha}=C_{\alpha}x.
\end{equation}

\par The augmented model used for controller design is therefore written as
\begin{equation}
\dot{z}=A_{\mathrm{aug}}z+B_{\mathrm{aug}}u,
\qquad
y=C_{\mathrm{aug}}z,
\end{equation}
with
\begin{equation}
A_{\mathrm{aug}}=
\begin{bmatrix}
A & 0\\
C_{\alpha} & 0
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
B_{\mathrm{aug}}=
\begin{bmatrix}
B\\
0
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
C_{\mathrm{aug}}=
\begin{bmatrix}
C & 0
\end{bmatrix}.
\end{equation}

\par The LQR design was then repeated for this augmented system. The quadratic cost becomes
\begin{equation}
J=\int_0^{\infty}\left(z^{T}Q_{r}z+R_{r}u^{2}\right)\,dt,
\end{equation}
where the last diagonal term of $Q_r$ penalizes the new integrator state. The resulting 
augmented gain is
\begin{equation}
K_{\mathrm{aug}}=
\begin{bmatrix}
K_x & K_i
\end{bmatrix},
\end{equation}
leading to the control law
\begin{equation}
u=-K_{\mathrm{aug}}\hat{z}=-K_x\hat{x}-K_i\xi_{\alpha}.
\end{equation}

\par This extra term is precisely what forces the mean value of $\alpha$ back to zero in the 
presence of constant biases or disturbances. In practical terms, if $\alpha$ remains offset 
from zero, the integrator state continues to grow, causing the control action to increase until 
the offset is rejected.

The Simulink implementation remained structurally unchanged (\ref{fig:lqg}). The same controller block used 
previously for the LQG design was preserved, and only the matrices inside the block were replaced 
by their augmented versions. In particular, the controller realization becomes
\begin{equation}
A_c=A_{\mathrm{aug}}-B_{\mathrm{aug}}K_{\mathrm{aug}}-L_{\mathrm{aug}}C_{\mathrm{aug}},
\qquad
B_c=L_{\mathrm{aug}},
\qquad
C_c=-K_{\mathrm{aug}},
\qquad
D_c=0.
\end{equation}

For implementation, the estimator for the original five physical states was kept, and the gain 
was extended as
\begin{equation}
L_{\mathrm{aug}}=
\begin{bmatrix}
L \\
1 \quad 0
\end{bmatrix},
\end{equation}
so that the new controller could still be simulated in the same Simulink architecture (\ref{fig:lqg}).
\subsubsection{Simulation Study - Influence of the Integrator Weight}

\par After defining the augmented model, a first simulation (Fig. \ref{fig:q11}) was performed to evaluate
 the effect of the integrator weight in the LQR cost function. In this study, the last 
 diagonal term of $Q_r$ was varied while the remaining regulator and estimator parameters were 
 kept unchanged. This allowed us to assess how strongly the controller should penalize the 
 accumulated error in $\alpha$.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.4\linewidth]{Body/Images/Q11_Qr_alpha_int.pdf}
    \caption{Simulation results with $\alpha$-integrator for different values of the integrator weight in $Q_r$.}
    \label{fig:q11_qr_alpha_int}
\end{figure}

\par As shown in Figure \ref{fig:q11_qr_alpha_int}, all tested configurations stabilize the system and 
eliminate the residual offset in $\alpha$. However, the choice of the integrator weight affects 
the transient response. Increasing the weight associated with the integral state generally makes 
the return of $\alpha$ to the origin faster, but also leads to a more aggressive response, with 
larger oscillations and stronger coupling into $\beta$. Therefore, this parameter introduces the 
expected trade-off between offset rejection speed and closed-loop smoothness.

\subsubsection{Simulation with Constant Disturbance}

\par To verify that the added integral action was effectively capable of rejecting constant biases, 
a second simulation was performed including a constant disturbance of $0.1$~V at the input (Fig. \ref{fig:q11_1}).

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.4\linewidth]{Body/Images/Q11_alpha_integrator.pdf}
    \caption{Simulation response of the LQG controller with $\alpha$-integrator under a constant disturbance of $0.1$~V.}
    \label{fig:q11_alpha_integrator_dist}
\end{figure}

\par The results in Fig.~\ref{fig:q11_alpha_integrator_dist} show that the controlled system is still 
able to drive the states back to the neighbourhood of the origin despite the persistent 
perturbation. This confirms the main purpose of the integral action: a 
constant disturbance produces a non-zero accumulated error, which in turn increases 
the control effort until the disturbance is compensated. In other words, the augmented 
controller no longer accepts a steady-state offset in $\alpha$ as an equilibrium condition.

\subsubsection{Experimental Validation without External Disturbance}

\par After the simulation analysis, an experimental test was conducted on the real setup 
using the $\alpha$-integrator, with integrator weight equal to $1$ and the remaining $Q_r$ 
parameters kept at their default values. The purpose of this experiment was to verify whether 
the behaviour predicted in simulation could be reproduced on the real plant even in the absence 
of an external perturbation.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.4\linewidth]{Body/Images/lqg_integrator.pdf}
    \caption{Experimental response of the LQG controller with $\alpha$-integrator and no external perturbation.}
    \label{fig:lqg_integrator_exp}
\end{figure}

\par As shown in Figure \ref{fig:lqg_integrator_exp}, the controller succeeds in keeping the mean 
position of the system around the origin, confirming that the arm no longer drifts to a non-zero 
equilibrium point. Nevertheless, the response still exhibits a visible oscillatory regime 
in both $\alpha$ and $\beta$, together with a periodic control action. 
This indicates that the integral action successfully removes the steady-state drift, but does not 
by itself solve the oscillatory behaviour caused by the actuator nonlinearity, namely the motor 
deadzone.

\subsubsection{Experimental Validation under Disturbance}

\par A final experimental test was then carried out with an external perturbation, in order to 
validate on the real setup the disturbance-rejection capability previously observed in simulation.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.4\linewidth]{Body/Images/lqg_integrator_perturb.pdf}
    \caption{Experimental response of the LQG controller with $\alpha$-integrator under external perturbation.}
    \label{fig:lqg_integrator_perturb_exp}
\end{figure}

\par The results in Figure \ref{fig:lqg_integrator_perturb_exp} show that the perturbation causes 
an initial large deviation, especially in $\alpha$, but the controller is able to drive the 
system back toward zero mean afterwards. This experimental result is particularly 
important because it confirms that the benefit of the added integrator is not limited to the 
simulation environment. Even in the real setup, where actuator nonlinearities and 
unmodelled effects are present, the controller no longer allows a persistent 
offset in $\alpha$ after a disturbance.

\par Overall, both the simulation and experimental results validate the introduction 
of the $\alpha$-integrator as an effective solution to the steady-state drift problem. 
However, the persistent oscillatory behaviour observed in the real system shows that integral 
action alone is not sufficient to guarantee a smooth regulation of the arm angle. 
This motivates the introduction of an additional deadzone compensator, discussed in the next 
subsection.

\subsection{Motor Deadzone Characterization and Compensation}

\par To quantify the motor deadzone, a voltage ramp was applied to the actuator to identify the 
specific threshold required to initiate motion. Experimental results yielded a deadzone half-width 
of $\pm0.34$ [V]. It is noted that preliminary measurements indicated a half-width of approximately $\pm0.20$ [V]; 
however, these initial data points were not formally recorded. It is critical to acknowledge that this parameter is 
subject to stochastic variations influenced by environmental factors, such as operating temperature. For the subsequent
 simulations and the design of the deadzone compensator, the value of $\pm0.20$ [V] was adopted.

 \begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.4\linewidth]{Body/Images/Calculo_Deadzone-sentido-2.pdf}
    \caption{Motor deadzone measurement}
    \label{fig:motor deadzone}
\end{figure}

\par The motor was modeled within the Simulink environment as a first-order system with a time constant $\tau = 0.01$ [s]. 
The model incorporates several non-linearities and constraints, including a voltage input saturation of $\pm5$ [V], output angular 
constraints of $\pm45^\circ$ for $\alpha$ and $\pm15^\circ$ for $\beta$, and a deadzone half-width of $\pm0.20$ [V]. A simulation 
was performed including the implemented $\alpha$ integrator to evaluate the uncompensated system response prior to the integration 
of the deadzone compensator. The values used for the regulator were $Q_r = diag([100,0,1,0,0,15])$ and $Rr = 0.01$; the resulting trajectory is illustrated below:

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.4\linewidth]{Body/Images/final_no_deadzone.pdf}
    \caption{Controlled system feedback}
    \label{fig:no_compensator}
\end{figure}

\par A deadzone compensation algorithm was developed to mitigate these nonlinear effects. The control logic determines
 the polarity of the input voltage, $u$, and applies an instantaneous offset of $+0.20$ [V] or $-0.20$ [V], respectively. 
 This effectively forces the control signal to bypass the deadzone region. This logic was realized via a \texttt{MATLAB Function} 
 block and integrated into the primary Simulink diagram. The results are present in the following plot:

 \begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.4\linewidth]{Body/Images/final_deadzone.pdf}
    \caption{Controlled system feedback with deadzone compensator}
    \label{fig:with_compensator}
\end{figure}

\par Following the implementation of the compensator, further simulations were conducted. The results demonstrate that 
the angle signals exhibit a reduced amplitude and the characteristic ``voltage jumping'' is clearly visible in the input signal.
 Overall, the deadzone is effectively bypassed, leading to superior control authority and enhanced system performance.
